# AwakenArts Relationship Strategy

**Status: proposed, 2026-06-29.** This is a first articulation, not a decided position — it belongs to the Publishing Track (per the Implementation Log's "Two tracks until Dragon's Reader is proven" section), which as of this writing has no architecture, spec, or log entry of its own. This document is meant to be argued with, not adopted by default.

Susan's question was: what relationship do we want people to have with AwakenArts after each of five thresholds — one visit, one Figure Edition read, one Collection purchased, one workshop attended, one retreat attended. The honest answer is that these are five different relationships, not one relationship at five volumes. Treating them as the same thing turned up the louder would push AwakenArts toward exactly the kind of marketing escalation its own foundation resists — "recognition through attentive engagement, not explanation" doesn't stop applying once money or a calendar is involved.

A useful existing reference point: the Collection page and Encounters index already carry a five-step graphic — Longing, Recognition, The Figure, The Word, The Path — describing how a visitor moves through the Collection itself. It doesn't map one-to-one onto Susan's five thresholds below, but the instinct behind it is the right one to keep: name what's true at each step, in AwakenArts' own vocabulary, rather than borrowing the language of funnels and stages from outside it.

---

## 1. After one visit

**What's actually happened.** A stranger has spent a few minutes with the homepage, possibly Collection or Encounters, and left — no email given, nothing bought, no account created.

**The relationship we want.** Not "a lead." Someone who has had a first, unclaimed encounter with a way of seeing — image and poem read as one thing, offered without being explained to death. The right outcome is that they remember the *feeling* of the work and the name AwakenArts, and carry a sense that there's more, without anything having asked them for a commitment yet. This is the one threshold where almost nothing should be asked of the visitor — the current site's restraint here (no popup, no early email gate) is correct and worth protecting, not "fixing" with a more aggressive ask.

**What earns the next visit.** Curiosity that wasn't fully resolved. If the homepage answered every question, there'd be no reason to come back. The recommendation from the Platform Presentation Evaluation — surfacing the differentiation claim (the Matthew 13:34 grounding) earlier — supports this relationship rather than working against it: a visitor who glimpses *why this is different* sooner is more likely to return on their own terms, not because they were chased.

---

## 2. After reading one Figure Edition

**What's actually happened.** Someone has completed an entire work — image, poem, and reflection as one piece — start to finish. This is the first moment a visitor has actually practiced the recognition the rest of the site only gestures toward.

**The relationship we want.** A shift from visitor to *reader* — someone who now understands AwakenArts experientially, not just descriptively. This is the most important relationship in the whole sequence to get right, because it's the first time trust has been earned by the work itself rather than promised by copy about the work. The right outcome is that they want to read the next one, and that wanting is met immediately rather than left to find its own way back to Collection.

**What this relationship is not.** It is not yet a customer relationship, and treating it like one too quickly — a hard sell into Collection ownership right after one free Edition — would mismatch the trust actually present. What's appropriate here is an invitation to continue the same relationship they're already in: hearing when the next Figure Edition exists, framed as continuation of the reading itself, not as a marketing list. This is the Editions Acquire moment the Implementation Specification's Phase 6 already anticipates and the Presentation Evaluation named as the single highest-impact gap — this document arrives at the same conclusion from the relationship side rather than the conversion side, which is a good sign it's the right call.

---

## 3. After purchasing one Collection

**What's actually happened.** Someone has decided the body of work, not just one piece of it, is worth owning. This is a different order of commitment than reading a free Edition — money has changed hands for the whole.

**The relationship we want.** Steward, not customer. Someone who has bought a Collection isn't done being sold to in the ordinary sense — they've already decided AwakenArts matters enough to hold onto in full. The right relationship from here is one of being trusted with more: continuity (what's coming next, in the same unhurried voice as everything else), and access to material that rewards owning the whole rather than sampling pieces — facilitator-style materials, the "how it's used" depth the Collection page already gestures toward (Reflection, Discussion, Teaching, Workshops, Journaling) but doesn't yet deliver on for someone who already owns everything.

**What to avoid.** Treating a Collection purchase as the finish line and going quiet. The risk on this site as it exists today isn't over-asking after a purchase — it's that nothing currently happens after one, because no purchase mechanism exists yet at all. When it does, the design choice that matters most is making sure "thank you for buying" isn't the last thing this relationship ever hears from AwakenArts.

---

## 4. After attending a workshop

**What's actually happened.** Someone has practiced the work in a guided, likely group, setting — facilitated discussion or teaching built around one or more Figure Editions, not solitary reading.

**The relationship we want.** Practitioner, not just reader. A workshop relationship is fundamentally social in a way the first three aren't — this person has used the language of the work out loud, with other people, under guidance. The right outcome is that they leave capable of carrying the method forward themselves: as someone who recommends AwakenArts to the next group they're part of, or as a future facilitator. This is the threshold where AwakenArts starts to multiply through people rather than through pages, which is also why Workshops being currently undiscoverable on the live site (flagged in the Presentation Evaluation, Section 8) matters more than it might first appear — it's not just a missing page, it's a missing relationship-tier that the rest of the site has no path toward at all right now.

**What this relationship asks of AwakenArts.** Continuity of facilitation quality and materials, not just continuity of content. A workshop participant's trust is partly in Susan as a person, more directly than a solitary reader's trust is — this relationship should feel personally hosted, not platform-mediated.

---

## 5. After a retreat

**What's actually happened.** Sustained, immersive, likely multi-day engagement — the deepest commitment of time, presence, and trust in the sequence.

**The relationship we want.** Formed companion. By this point the relationship isn't really about AwakenArts' published works at all anymore — it's about a shared, lived experience of the method and the foundation behind it. The right outcome is something closer to ongoing community than ongoing customer: people who return to future retreats, who stay in contact with each other and not only with AwakenArts, and who may become the next generation of facilitators themselves. This is the threshold furthest from "marketing" in any ordinary sense — it should feel earned rather than offered, and the relationship strategy here is mostly about not getting in the way: minimal asks, maximal trust already assumed.

**Where this leads.** This is the natural top of the sequence the Implementation Log's expansion strategy already names — Collections, Workshops, and Retreats are listed there in that order for a reason, and the relationship at each tier deepens in the same order the capabilities are sequenced to be built. Retreats shouldn't be designed as "Workshops, but longer" any more than a Collection purchase should be designed as "an Edition, but bigger" — each threshold changes the kind of relationship, not just its size.

---

## How the relationship progresses

What stays constant across all five thresholds is the core relational promise already stated in the Foundation: recognition through attentive engagement, not explanation, and an invitation rather than pressure. That promise should hold exactly as true for someone deciding whether to buy a Collection as for someone arriving at the homepage for the first time.

What changes is the *mode* of the relationship, not its tone: solitary and unclaimed (one visit), solitary and proven (one Edition read), invested and stewarded (one Collection owned), social and practiced (one workshop), and formed and communal (one retreat). Each step deepens the relationship's depth of commitment without ever asking AwakenArts to become louder, faster, or more conventional to get there — which is itself the test for whether any future tactic belongs here: if it would only make sense for a louder brand, it isn't a strategy for this one.
